The newsroom desk pulls publisher RSS (Nature peptides / peptide hormones) so editors see what actually published — not what a Telegram channel remixed.

That feed is a **lead list**, not a stack of ready-to-publish “stack protocols.”

## What a typical pull looks like

Recent desk items included synthesis methods, GPCR ligand work, antimicrobial peptide optimization, imaging peptides, and peptide-hormone signaling papers. Most are **methods / structural / preclinical** stories. Useful for literacy. Useless as gym folklore.

## How to read one without self-owning

1. **Find the model system** — cell, animal, human, or pure chemistry. Upgrade nothing.
2. **Find the claim boundary** — “inhibits interaction in vitro” ≠ “fixes your elbows.”
3. **Ignore the press-release title voice** — journal titles can still be dense; influencer captions cannot.
4. **Check whether the molecule is FDA-context, RUO, or neither** — see [Approved vs research compounds](/guides/approved-vs-research-compounds).
5. **Park dosing curiosity** — Amino Brief will not translate papers into human protocols.
