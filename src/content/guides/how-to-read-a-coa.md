A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is paperwork that claims: “this lot was tested, here’s what we found.” It’s only useful if it maps to **the vial in your hand**.

## Check these fields first

1. **Compound identity** — name, and ideally a method that supports identity (HPLC, MS, etc.).
2. **Lot / batch number** — must match the label.
3. **Purity** — often HPLC %. Know what “purity” does and doesn’t prove.
4. **Test date + lab** — who ran it, when.
5. **Methods language** — vague “tested for purity” with no method is a red flag.

## Classic ways people get played

- Generic COA recycled across lots
- Purity number with no chromatogram or method
- Lot on PDF ≠ lot on vial
- “99% pure” marketing graphic that isn’t a lab report

## What purity is not

High HPLC purity does not prove:

- clinical safety
- correct dosing for humans
- that the compound does what TikTok said

It supports **identity/purity claims for that tested lot** — full stop.

## Practical habit

Archive the COA with the lot number. If a source can’t produce lot-linked documentation, treat them like a random tub of mystery powder with better branding.
